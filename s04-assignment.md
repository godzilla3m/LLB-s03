### Q1. Define confession? Why is it that confession made to a police officer or in police custody is not admissible in evidence?  

**Introduction**  
Confession means a statement made by a person admitting guilt of an offence. It is a direct acknowledgment of facts that constitute a crime. For example, if a person says, “I stole the money,” that is a confession. In law, confessions are important because they can prove guilt without further evidence.  

**Main Body**  
- **Definition of Confession**  
  A confession is an admission made by an accused person suggesting that he committed the offence. It is different from an admission in civil cases because it directly relates to criminal liability.  

- **Legal Position in India**  
  According to the Indian Evidence Act, Sections 24 to 30 deal with confessions. A confession must be voluntary and free from pressure. If it is obtained by threat, inducement, or promise, it is not valid.  

- **Confession to Police Officer**  
  Section 25 of the Indian Evidence Act clearly states that a confession made to a police officer is not admissible in evidence. This is because police officers may use force, threats, or pressure to obtain such statements. For example, if an accused admits guilt during interrogation, the court will not accept it as proof.  

- **Confession in Police Custody**  
  Section 26 of the Act says that a confession made while in police custody is also not admissible unless it is made before a magistrate. The reason is to protect the accused from torture or coercion. Only a magistrate can ensure that the confession is voluntary.  

- **Reason for Exclusion**  
  The law excludes such confessions to safeguard the rights of individuals and to prevent misuse of power by police. It ensures that convictions are based on fair and reliable evidence, not forced statements.  

**Conclusion** 
In short, confession means admitting guilt of a crime. But confessions made to police officers or in police custody are not admissible because they may be obtained by pressure or fear. The law requires that confessions must be voluntary and preferably recorded before a magistrate to be valid. This rule protects justice and prevents wrongful convictions.

### Q2. “The art of cross-examination is a double-edged weapon” critically comment. State what questions cannot be asked in cross-examination?

**Introduction**

Cross-examination, known in Hindi as **प्रति-परीक्षा** (Prati-pariksha), is defined under **Section 137** of the Indian Evidence Act. It is the process where the opposite party questions a witness to test their truthfulness and memory. It is considered the most effective way to discover the truth in a courtroom. However, it is a highly skilled task that requires great care.

**Main Body**

**1. The Double-Edged Weapon**
The phrase "double-edged weapon" (**दोधारी तलवार**) means that while cross-examination can destroy the opponent's case, it can also hurt the person using it if not handled properly.
* **The Sharp Edge (Benefit):** A skillful lawyer can use it to expose lies, show contradictions, and prove that a witness is not reliable. It helps the court see the hidden truth.
* **The Backfire (Risk):** If a lawyer is not careful and asks a "why" question or a question they don't know the answer to, the witness might give a detailed explanation that makes the opponent's case even stronger. Instead of breaking the witness, the lawyer might end up helping them.

**2. Questions That Cannot Be Asked**
The law does not give a lawyer total freedom to harass a witness. The Indian Evidence Act lists specific restrictions:
* **Indecent and Scandalous Questions (Section 151):** The court can stop questions that are indecent (**अश्लील**) or scandalous, unless they relate directly to the main facts of the case.
* **Questions Intended to Insult or Annoy (Section 152):** A lawyer is forbidden from asking questions solely to insult (**अपमानित करना**) or annoy the witness.
* **Questions Without Reasonable Grounds (Section 149):** A lawyer cannot attack a witness's character unless they have a solid reason or evidence to support that specific accusation.
* **Victim’s Character in Sexual Offenses (Section 146):** In cases of rape or sexual assault, it is strictly forbidden to ask questions about the victim's previous sexual experience or general "immoral character."

**Conclusion**

Cross-examination is an essential tool for justice, but it must be used ethically. It requires a balance of boldness and caution. While it serves to protect "human dignity" by exposing false accusations, the court acts as a shield to ensure the witness is not subjected to unnecessary mental pain or character assassination. Under the law, the goal is always to find the truth, not to win by bullying.

## Labour law - 2

### Q1. Explain the rights to payment of maternity benefits?

**Introduction**

The right to maternity benefit is a fundamental social security right for women employees in India. This right is protected under the **Maternity Benefit Act, 1961**. The main goal is to ensure that a woman does not have to choose between her job and her child. It protects her "human dignity" and health during and after pregnancy.

**Main Body**

**1. Right to Paid Leave**
Every eligible woman has the right to be paid for the period she is absent from work due to childbirth.
* **Duration:** A woman can claim maternity benefit for a maximum of **26 weeks**. Out of these, not more than 8 weeks can be taken before the expected date of delivery.
* **Rate of Payment:** The benefit must be paid at the rate of the **average daily wage** for the period of her actual absence.

**2. Right to Medical Bonus**
If the employer does not provide free medical care to the woman, she has the legal right to receive a **Medical Bonus**. This is a lump sum payment (currently around ₹3,500) given to cover the expenses related to pregnancy.

**3. Right to Nursing Breaks**
After returning to work, a woman has the right to take two "nursing breaks" (**शिशु को दूध पिलाने के लिए अंतराल**) daily until the child reaches the age of 15 months. These breaks are in addition to the regular rest intervals and are counted as part of her working hours.

**4. Protection Against Dismissal**
It is illegal for an employer to discharge or dismiss a woman because she is absent on maternity leave. This is a very strong right that prevents "victimization" of pregnant employees.

**5. Right to Light Work**
For a period of one month before she goes on maternity leave, a woman has the right to request that she not be given "arduous work" or work that involves long hours of standing. The employer must provide her with lighter duties to ensure the safety of the mother and child.

**Example:** Suppose a woman working in a private company in Raebareli is in her 7th month of pregnancy. She has the right to tell her boss that she can no longer lift heavy files or stand for 8 hours. The boss must legally give her desk work and cannot cut her salary for this change.

**Conclusion**

The rights to maternity benefit are not just financial payments; they are rights to "social justice." They ensure that a woman can experience motherhood without the fear of losing her income or her job. By providing paid leave, medical bonuses, and job security, the law honors the biological role of women while maintaining their status as productive members of the workforce. This protects the "free development of personality" for both the mother and her child.

### Q2. Elaborate the time of payment of wages under the payments of wages act?

**Introduction**

The **Payment of Wages Act, 1936** was passed to ensure that workers are not only paid the correct amount but also paid on time. The Hindi term for "time of payment" is **भुगतान का समय** (Bhugtan ka samay). Many employers used to delay salaries for months, causing great hardship to workers. **Section 5** of the Act specifically deals with the rules regarding when an employer must legally hand over the wages to their employees.

**Main Body**

**1. The 7th Day and 10th Day Rule**
The Act divides establishments into two categories based on the number of workers to decide the deadline for payment:
* **Less than 1000 workers:** In factories or establishments where fewer than one thousand people are employed, the wages must be paid before the expiry of the **7th day** of the following month.
* **1000 or more workers:** In larger establishments with one thousand or more employees (like large railway projects or big factories), the employer has a little more time. They must pay before the expiry of the **10th day** of the following month.

**2. Payment in Case of Termination**
If an employee is fired, dismissed, or their employment is terminated by the employer, the law is even stricter. In such cases, the wages earned by the employee must be paid before the expiry of the **second working day** from the day their employment was terminated.

**Example:** If a worker is dismissed on a Monday, the employer must settle all their dues by the end of Wednesday.

**3. Payment on a Working Day**
All payments of wages must be made on a **working day**. The employer cannot call workers to the factory on a public holiday or a Sunday just to collect their salary.

**4. Wage Periods**
The person responsible for payment must fix "wage periods" (e.g., daily, weekly, or monthly). However, no wage period can exceed **one month**. This prevents employers from setting a "yearly" wage period to delay payments.

**Conclusion**

The rules regarding the time of payment are designed to provide financial stability to the labor class. By fixing the 7th or 10th day as the deadline, the law ensures that a worker can plan their household expenses and maintain their "human dignity." Timely payment prevents workers from falling into the trap of high-interest loans, thus supporting the "free development of their personality" and ensuring a fair relationship between the employer and the employee.

## Code of civil procedure & law of limitation - 2

### Q1. What do you understand by substantial question of law’ on which second appeal lies in the High Court under section 100 of the CPC 1908

**Introduction**

Section 100 of the Civil Procedure Code (CPC), 1908, provides the right to a **Second Appeal** in the High Court. Unlike a first appeal, where the court can re-examine both facts and law, a second appeal is very restricted. It can only be filed if the High Court is satisfied that the case involves a **Substantial Question of Law**. The goal is to ensure that the High Court focuses on correcting major legal errors rather than small factual disputes.

**Main Body**

**1. What is a Substantial Question of Law?**
A "question of law" becomes "substantial" when it is not just a simple legal point, but a significant one that deeply affects the rights of the parties. It is a question that is debatable and has not already been settled by the Supreme Court or the High Court.

**2. Criteria for a Substantial Question**
For a point to be considered a substantial question of law, it must meet these conditions:
* It must not be previously decided by a higher court.
* It must have a direct effect on the outcome of the case.
* It should be a matter where there is a conflict of legal opinions between different courts.

**3. Distinction from Question of Fact**
A second appeal cannot be filed just because a person disagrees with the evidence (facts). 
* **Question of Fact:** Whether a person was present at a meeting is a factual matter.
* **Substantial Question of Law:** Whether a specific document is legally valid under the Registration Act is a legal matter.

**Example:** Suppose there is a property dispute in Raebareli. If the lower court misinterprets a section of the Transfer of Property Act in a way that contradicts a Supreme Court ruling, this is a **Substantial Question of Law**. The High Court will then hear the appeal to correct this legal mistake.

**Conclusion**

The requirement of a "Substantial Question of Law" acts as a filter. It ensures that the High Court's time is used for serious legal issues that impact "human dignity" and the "free development" of the law. By limiting second appeals to major legal points, the CPC maintains a balance between providing justice and preventing endless litigation over small factual details.

### Q2. Legal disabilities stop the continuous running of time” Discuss the related provisions of the limitation act 1963? 

**Introduction**

The **Limitation Act, 1963** (परिसीमा अधिनियम) sets a specific time limit within which a person must file a lawsuit. If they miss this deadline, they lose the right to go to court. However, the law recognizes that some people are not in a position to understand or exercise their rights. This is called **Legal Disability** (विधिक निर्योग्यता). The provisions related to this are found in **Sections 6, 7, 8, and 9** of the Act. They ensure that "human dignity" is protected for those who cannot protect themselves.

**Main Body**

**1. Who is Legally Disabled? (Section 6)**
According to the Act, there are three categories of people who are considered legally disabled:
* **Minors:** Anyone under the age of 18.
* **Insane Persons:** People of unsound mind.
* **Idiots:** A specific legal term for persons with severe mental disabilities.

If a person belongs to any of these categories at the time their right to sue arises, the "limitation clock" does not start. It only starts ticking once the disability ends (e.g., when a minor turns 18 or an insane person becomes healthy).

**2. The Rule of Continuous Running (Section 9)**
It is vital to note **Section 9**, which states: "Where once time has begun to run, no subsequent disability or inability to institute a suit or make an application stops it." 
This means the disability must exist at the **exact moment** the cause of action starts. If the time has already started running and then a person becomes disabled, the clock will **not** stop.

**Example:** Imagine a 12-year-old child in Raebareli has their land taken away. Usually, they would have 12 years to sue. Because of the "Legal Disability," the 12-year period will only start when the child turns 18 (effectively, he can sue up to age 30.). However, if the child was 19 when the land was taken and then later became mentally ill, the 12-year limit would keep running because the time had already started. (For immovable property, the standard limitation is 12 years).



**3. Special Exceptions (Section 8)**
Section 8 limits the extension. It says that once the disability ends, the person has a maximum of **three years** to file the suit, even if the original limitation period was longer.

**Conclusion**

The concept of legal disability is a humane exception to a strict rule. While the law wants cases to be filed quickly to ensure certainty, it also wants to protect the "free development of personality" for those who are vulnerable. By pausing the clock for minors and the mentally ill, the Limitation Act ensures that justice is not denied to those who are physically or mentally unable to ask for it on time.

## BNSS - 2

### Q1. What do you understand by trial of an offence? Discuss in detail the trial procedure before court of session? BNSS

**Introduction**

A **Trial** (**विचारण**) is the most important stage of a criminal case. It is a judicial proceeding where a Judge examines the evidence and hears arguments to determine if the person accused of a crime is guilty (**दोषी**) or innocent (**निर्दोष**). Under the **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023**, which has replaced the CrPC, the trial procedure has been updated to ensure faster justice. A "Sessions Trial" is conducted for very serious crimes, such as murder or dacoity, which are punishable with death or life imprisonment.

**Main Body**

**1. Meaning of Trial**
A trial starts after the police finish their investigation and file a charge sheet. It is a formal process where the court strictly follows the law to ensure the "human dignity" of the accused is protected and they get a fair chance to defend themselves.

**2. Trial Procedure Before a Court of Session (Chapter XVIII of BNSS)**
The procedure follows these specific steps:

* **Opening of the Case (Section 249):** The Public Prosecutor (**लोक अभियोजक**) starts the case by describing the charges against the accused and mentioning the evidence they will use to prove the guilt.
* **Discharge (Section 250):** If the Judge, after hearing the prosecutor and the accused, feels there is not enough ground to proceed, they can discharge (**कार्यमुक्त**) the accused. This happens if the evidence is too weak even to start a trial.
* **Framing of Charge (Section 251):** If the Judge believes a crime was likely committed, they write down a formal **Charge** (**आरोप**). The charge is read and explained to the accused in simple language.
* **Plea of Guilty (Section 252):** The Judge asks the accused, "Do you plead guilty?" If the accused says yes, the Judge can convict them immediately without a full trial.
* **Evidence for Prosecution (Section 254):** If the accused says they are not guilty, the trial continues. The Prosecutor brings witnesses, and the defense lawyer cross-examines them. 
* **Evidence for Defence (Section 256):** After the prosecution finishes, the accused is given a chance to bring their own witnesses and evidence to prove their innocence.



**Example:** Imagine a case in Raebareli where a person is accused of a major robbery. The case is "committed" to the Sessions Court because it is a serious crime. If the Judge finds that the police didn't find any fingerprints or witnesses, the Judge might **discharge** the person under Section 250. However, if there is a CCTV clip, the Judge will **frame a charge** and start the full trial.

**Conclusion**

The trial procedure under BNSS ensures that "social justice" is delivered through a balanced process. By giving the accused a chance to hear the charges and present their defense, the law protects the "free development of personality" and prevents innocent people from being punished. The Sessions Trial remains the most detailed form of trial to handle the most severe crimes in society.

### Q. 2. What is warrant case trial? How does a warrant case based on public report and other than police report take place? BNSS

**Introduction**

In the Indian criminal justice system, cases are categorized based on the seriousness of the offense. A **Warrant Case** (**वारंट मामला**) refers to an offense that carries a severe punishment. Under the **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023**, the procedure for a warrant case trial is designed to be more detailed than a summons case to ensure a fair trial for the accused. The law provides two distinct paths for these trials: one starting from a police investigation (Police Report) and the other starting from a direct complaint to a Magistrate (Private Complaint).

**Definition of a Warrant Case**

Under BNSS, a warrant case is defined as a case relating to an offense punishable with death, imprisonment for life, or imprisonment for a term exceeding **two years**. Because the stakes are high—involving a long prison sentence or even death—the law requires a strict procedure for framing charges and examining witnesses.

**Trial Based on a Police Report (Public Report)**

When a case is instituted on a "Police Report" (**पुलिस रिपोर्ट पर संस्थित**), it means the police registered an FIR, investigated the matter, and filed a charge sheet in court. The procedure (Sections 261 to 266 of BNSS) is as follows:

* **Supply of Documents:** The Magistrate must ensure that the accused is provided with copies of the police report and other relevant documents so they can prepare a defense.
* **Discharge (Section 262):** If the Magistrate considers the charge sheet and documents and finds that the case against the accused is "groundless," they shall discharge (**कार्यमुक्त**) the accused.
* **Framing of Charge (Section 263):** If there is enough evidence to suggest the accused committed the crime, the Magistrate frames a formal written charge. It is read to the accused, who is asked if they plead guilty.
* **Evidence for Prosecution:** If the accused claims a trial, the Magistrate takes all evidence produced by the prosecution. The witnesses are examined and cross-examined.
* **Evidence for Defence:** After the prosecution is done, the accused is called upon to enter their defense and produce any witnesses or documents.

**Trial Other than on a Police Report (Private Complaint)**

This occurs when a citizen files a direct complaint (**शिकायत**) to a Magistrate. The procedure (Sections 267 to 271 of BNSS) is slightly different because there has been no prior police investigation.

* **Evidence Before Charge (Section 267):** Unlike a police case, the Magistrate first hears the prosecution's witnesses *before* deciding whether to frame a charge. This is a "pre-trial" stage to see if there is a prima facie case.
* **Discharge (Section 268):** If, after hearing this evidence, the Magistrate finds that no case is made out, the accused is discharged.
* **Framing of Charge (Section 269):** If a case is made out, a formal charge is framed. 
* **Recall of Witnesses:** After the charge is framed, the accused has a special right to recall the prosecution witnesses for a second round of cross-examination. This is a unique feature of private complaint cases.



**Example: Theft in Raebareli**
If a person is accused of a major theft (punishable by 3 years), it is a warrant case. 
1. If the **Raebareli Police** catch the thief and file a charge sheet, the "Police Report" procedure is followed. 
2. If the police refuse to help and the victim files a case directly in the **Magistrate’s Court**, the "Other than Police Report" procedure is followed.

**Conclusion**

The distinction between the two types of warrant trials ensures that "human dignity" is maintained regardless of how the case reaches the court. By allowing a discharge if the case is groundless and giving the accused a chance to cross-examine witnesses, the BNSS upholds the principle of a fair trial. The detailed procedure acts as a safeguard against false accusations and ensures that serious punishments are only handed out after a thorough judicial examination.

## Property law and easement

### Q1. What do you mean by profit a prendre? What is a distinction between English and Indian Law?

**Introduction**

In property law, the right to use someone else's land can take different forms. One such important right is **Profit a Prendre**. The term is derived from French, meaning "to take a profit." In Hindi, it is known as **भूमि से लाभ लेने का अधिकार** (Bhumi se laabh lene ka adhikaar). While a regular easement (like a right of way) only allows you to *use* the land, a profit a prendre allows you to actually *take* something away from the land. This legal concept ensures that natural resources like timber, fish, or minerals can be shared or sold as specific property rights.

**Main Body**

**1. Meaning of Profit a Prendre**
A profit a prendre is a right to enter the land of another person and take some profit of the soil, or a portion of the soil itself, for one's own use. The "profit" here does not mean money; it means physical things produced by nature on the land.

**Examples of Profit a Prendre:**
* **Grazing:** The right to let your cattle eat the grass on a neighbor's field.
* **Fishing:** The right to catch and take away fish from a pond belonging to another.
* **Mining:** The right to dig for and take away coal or sand from another's land.
* **Timber:** The right to cut and take wood from someone else's forest.



**2. Distinction between English and Indian Law**
While both legal systems recognize this right, they categorize it differently.

**A. Treatment as a Separate Right**
* **English Law:** In England, an **Easement** and a **Profit a Prendre** are treated as two distinct species of rights. An easement only gives a right of "use" (like walking over a path) but never the right to "take" the soil. Taking something is always called a profit a prendre.
* **Indian Law:** In India, the **Indian Easements Act, 1882** simplifies this. Under **Section 4**, the definition of an "Easement" explicitly includes the right to take a part of the soil or anything growing on it. Therefore, in India, a profit a prendre is simply considered a **type of easement**.

**B. Rights "In Gross"**
* **English Law:** In England, a profit a prendre can be held **"in gross."** This means a person can have the right to take fish from a river even if they do not own any land nearby.
* **Indian Law:** Under the Indian Easements Act, an easement (which includes profit) must always have a **Dominant Heritage** (the land that benefits) and a **Servient Heritage** (the land that provides). If Prateek has a right to take wood from a forest but does not own any land that benefits from that wood, it is not an "easement" under the Act. It would instead be treated as a "grant" or a contract under the Transfer of Property Act.

**C. Essential Requirements**
* **In England:** A profit a prendre is often seen as an "interest in land" itself.
* **In India:** Because it is part of the Easements Act, it is seen as a "restrictive right" over someone else's ownership. The focus is on the relationship between two pieces of land.

**Comparison Table for Quick Reference:**

| Feature | English Law | Indian Law |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Classification** | Distinct from Easements | Included within "Easements" |
| **Rights "In Gross"** | Allowed (Personal right) | Not allowed as an Easement |
| **Nature of Right** | Right to "Take" only | Right to "Do" or "Take" |

**Conclusion**

To conclude, while the basic idea of "taking a profit" from another's land is the same in both countries, the legal structure differs. English law treats it as a unique and independent right that can belong to an individual personally. Indian law, through the Indian Easements Act, has merged it into the broader concept of easements to create a more unified system. For an LLB student, it is vital to remember that in India, for a profit a prendre to be a legal easement, it must benefit a specific piece of land owned by the person claiming the right. This ensures that land rights remain organized and tied to the "human dignity" and "utility" of property ownership.

### Q. 2. ‘Explain vested interest and contingent interest’ and bring out distinction between the two? What’s doctrine of election? What are the conditions in which a person is put to election?

**Introduction**

In the **Transfer of Property Act, 1882**, when property is transferred, the person receiving it gets an "interest" in it. This interest can be of two main types: **Vested Interest** (**निहित हित**) and **Contingent Interest** (**समाश्रित हित**). Understanding these is important to know exactly when a person becomes the legal owner.

**Main Body**

**1. Vested Interest (Section 19)**
A vested interest is an immediate right to the property. Even if the person does not get to use or "possess" the property right away, they are already the owner in the eyes of the law. 
* It is not defeated by the death of the person before they get possession. 
* It can be sold or inherited.

**Example:** Prateek’s father transfers a house to Prateek, but says "you will get possession only after I die." Here, Prateek has a **vested interest**. Even if Prateek dies before his father, the house will belong to Prateek’s heirs.



**2. Contingent Interest (Section 21)**
This interest depends on an **uncertain event**. If the event happens, the interest becomes vested. If the event does not happen, the person gets nothing.
* If the person dies before the event happens, they gain no rights.

**Example:** A person says to Prateek, "I will give you this land if you score 90% in your LLB exams." This is a **contingent interest**. If Prateek fails to get 90%, he gets no land.

**3. Distinction Table**

| Feature | Vested Interest | Contingent Interest |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Condition** | It is certain. | It depends on an uncertain event. |
| **Ownership** | Immediate legal ownership. | Ownership is just a "chance." |
| **Death** | Doesn't fail if the person dies. | Fails if the person dies before event. |
| **Transfer** | Can be sold or inherited. | Cannot be inherited until event occurs. |

**4. Doctrine of Election (Section 35)**
The **Doctrine of Election** (**निर्वाचन का सिद्धांत**) is based on the principle of equity. It says that you cannot "approbate and reprobate"—meaning you cannot accept the benefits of a deal and reject the burdens at the same time.

**Example:** A person transfers your car to a third party and, in the same document, gives you ₹5 Lakhs. You must **elect** (choose). If you take the ₹5 Lakhs, you must give up the car. You cannot keep both the car and the money.

**5. Conditions for Election**
A person is "put to election" only when:
* The transferor tries to give away property they do not own.
* In the same transaction, they give a benefit to the real owner of that property.
* The benefit is given directly in exchange for the property.

**Conclusion**

Vested and Contingent interests define the "when" of property ownership—whether it is certain or depends on luck. The Doctrine of Election ensures "social justice" by preventing people from taking unfair advantage of a legal document. For an LLB student, these concepts are the foundation of understanding how property rights move from one person to another in a fair and organized way.

## Company law - 2

### Q1. Explain the rule of majority shareholders. Are there any exceptions to this rule? Discuss.

**Introduction**

In Company Law, a company is managed based on the principle of democracy. Since a company is a separate legal entity, the decisions are made by the persons who hold the most power—the majority shareholders (**बहुमत शेयरधारक**). The fundamental rule is that the will of the majority prevails, and the court generally does not interfere in the internal management of a company. This is known as the "Rule of Majority." This principle ensures that the company can function smoothly without being stuck in constant legal battles over small disagreements.

NOTE: Unlike political democracy (one person, one vote), in companies it’s one share, one vote. A person with 10,000 shares has more influence than someone with 10. The analogy is used because decisions are typically made by voting, and the majority’s will prevails.

**Main Body**

**1. The Rule in Foss v. Harbottle (1843)**
The "Rule of Majority" was firmly established in the English case of **Foss v. Harbottle**. In this case, two shareholders sued the directors, claiming they had misused the company's property. The court dismissed the case, stating:
* **The Proper Plaintiff Rule:** If a wrong is done to the company, the company itself is the only "proper plaintiff" (**उचित वादी**) who can sue.
* **Majority Rule:** If the alleged wrong is something that the majority of shareholders can ratify (approve) in a general meeting, the court will not interfere.

**Example:** If a director makes a small technical mistake in a contract that the shareholders are happy to overlook, a single minority shareholder cannot drag the company to court. The majority has the right to decide to move on.



**2. Exceptions to the Rule (Protection of Minority)**
If the majority was allowed to do whatever they wanted, they could easily loot the company or ignore the rights of the minority. To prevent this "tyranny of the majority," the law provides several exceptions. In these cases, even a single shareholder can sue the company:

* **Ultra Vires Acts:** If the majority decides to do something that is beyond the legal powers of the company (outside the Memorandum of Association), it is **Ultra Vires** (**अधिकारतीत**). Any shareholder can stop this.
    * *Example:* If a textile company suddenly starts a real estate business without changing its objects clause, a minority shareholder can get an injunction.
* **Fraud on the Minority:** If the majority uses their power to steal the company's assets or take away the rights of the minority shareholders, it is considered fraud.
    * *Case Law:* **Cook v. Deeks (1916)** – The directors took a contract in their own names which should have belonged to the company. The court held that the majority cannot use their votes to "gift" themselves company property.
* **Acts Requiring a Special Majority:** Some decisions (like changing the Articles of Association) require a **Special Resolution** (75% votes). If the majority (say 51%) tries to pass such a decision by a simple majority, the minority can challenge it.
* **Oppression and Mismanagement (Sections 241-244):** Under the Indian Companies Act, 2013, if the affairs of the company are being conducted in a manner prejudicial to the public interest or oppressive (**उत्पीड़न**) to any member, the minority can approach the **National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT)**.
    * *Case Law:* **Shanti Prasad Jain v. Kalinga Tubes Ltd.** – The court clarified that "oppression" must involve a lack of probity and fair dealing which causes prejudice to the minority's rights as shareholders.



**Conclusion**

The rule of majority is essential for the "free development" and efficient running of a corporation. It prevents frivolous lawsuits and ensures that the business can make quick decisions. However, "human dignity" and "social justice" demand that this power is not absolute. The exceptions to the rule act as a safety valve, ensuring that the minority is not crushed by the majority. By allowing the NCLT to intervene in cases of oppression and fraud, Indian law maintains a balance between corporate democracy and the protection of individual shareholder rights.

### Q2. What do you understand by corporate social responsibility? Describe the statutory enforcement guidelines related to corporate social responsibility. (commercial law)

**Introduction**

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR), known in Hindi as **नैगम सामाजिक उत्तरदायित्व** (Naigam Samajik Uttardayitwa), is the concept that a company has a duty toward the society in which it operates. In India, CSR is not just a moral choice but a legal obligation under **Section 135** of the **Companies Act, 2013**. This law ensures that "corporate citizens" contribute to sustainable development and the "human dignity" of the common man by sharing a portion of their profits for social welfare.

**Main Body**

**1. Applicability of CSR**
The CSR provisions apply to every company (including its holding, subsidiary, or a foreign company with a branch in India) that meets any of these thresholds in the preceding financial year:
* **Net Worth:** ₹500 crore or more.
* **Turnover:** ₹1,000 crore or more.
* **Net Profit:** ₹5 crore or more.

**2. The CSR Committee and Policy**
Eligible companies must constitute a **CSR Committee** of the Board consisting of three or more directors, including at least one independent director. 
* **Role:** This committee formulates the CSR Policy and recommends the amount to be spent. 
* **Exception:** If the CSR obligation is less than **₹50 lakh**, the board can perform these functions directly without forming a separate committee.

**3. Statutory Spending Requirement**
The Board must ensure that the company spends, in every financial year, at least **2% of the average net profits** made during the three immediately preceding financial years. The activities must fall under **Schedule VII**, such as:
* Eradicating hunger and poverty.
* Promoting education and gender equality.
* Environmental sustainability and disaster management.

**Example:** If a tech firm in Raebareli makes an average net profit of ₹100 crore over three years, it must spend at least ₹2 crore on CSR activities like building local schools or planting trees.



**4. Statutory Enforcement and Penalties**
The law has moved from a "Comply or Explain" model to a more strict "Comply or be Penalized" system.
* **Transfer of Unspent Funds:** If a company fails to spend the amount, it must transfer the unspent money to a government-specified fund (like the PM Cares Fund) within six months. For **Ongoing Projects**, the money must be transferred to a special "Unspent CSR Account" within 30 days.
* **Penalties for Default:** If a company violates these rules, it faces a penalty of **twice the unspent amount** (up to ₹1 crore). Every officer in default can be fined up to **₹2 lakh**.
* **Reporting:** Companies must include an annual report on CSR in their Board’s Report and publish their CSR Policy on their official website.

**Conclusion**

CSR is a powerful tool for "social justice" in the commercial world. It bridges the gap between massive corporate wealth and the needs of the grassroots population. By enforcing strict guidelines for spending, reporting, and penalties, the Companies Act ensures that businesses do not just focus on profit but also on the "free development of personality" for the society that sustains them. This legal framework makes Indian corporations active partners in national growth and community welfare.
